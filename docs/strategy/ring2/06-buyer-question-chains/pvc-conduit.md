# Buyer Question Chain: PVC Conduit Fittings

> Ring 2, Task 6 | Sub-line: PVC Conduit
> Inputs: Ring 1 personas (Mark the Distributor, James the Contractor), competitive analysis, brand positioning
> Owner-confirmed seed topics: certifications, standards compliance, price, MOQ, shipping, lead time

## Personas Covered

- **PVC Distributor (Mark)** — Regional building materials distributor (Australia/SEA/Middle East), 3-8 retail locations, looking for long-term factory supply relationship. Cares most about: quality consistency, certifications, margin.
- **PVC Contractor (James)** — Electrical contractor / project engineer (North America/Australia), needs exact specs for active projects. Cares most about: spec compliance, lead time, dimension accuracy.

## How to Read This Document

Each stage maps to a buyer's mental state as they move from "never heard of Tianze" to "ready to send a PO." Questions are written in the buyer's own language (sourced from persona research). The chain is not linear — buyers skip stages, loop back, and different personas enter at different stages. James often enters at Stage 2 (he already knows what he needs; he's checking if you have it). Mark usually starts at Stage 1.

## Question Chain

### Stage 1: Awareness (First Encountering Tianze)

The buyer has landed on the site — from Google, Alibaba overflow, or a referral. They're doing a 10-second gut check: is this worth my time?

**Q1 (Mark): "Is this a real factory or another trading company?"**
This is the single most important question for distributors. They've been burned by traders who sourced from random factories, delivered inconsistent quality, then disappeared. Mark is scanning for: factory photos, production equipment, employee count, established year — anything that separates a manufacturer from a middleman.

**Q2 (James): "Do they make the specific fittings I need — Schedule 40/80, the right standard?"**
James doesn't care about your company story yet. He has a project spec sheet in hand and needs to know in 5 seconds whether your product range matches. If he can't find his spec, he's gone.

**Q3 (Mark): "What standards do they manufacture to? AS/NZS? ASTM? UL?"**
Not "do you have certificates" yet — that comes in Stage 2. At this point Mark just wants to know if the factory is even in the right ballpark for his market's regulatory requirements.

**Q4 (James): "Can they match this drawing / custom spec?"**
For non-standard projects, James needs to know if custom work is possible before investing time in a conversation.

**Content needed to answer Stage 1:**
- Homepage hero that immediately signals "manufacturer, not trader" (the bending machine story)
- Product catalog page with clear standards filtering (AS/NZS 2053, ASTM D2467, UL 651)
- Visible product range overview — bend angles, sizes, schedules — scannable in under 10 seconds
- OEM/custom capability statement visible within 1 click from homepage

---

### Stage 2: Comparison (Evaluating Against 2-4 Alternatives)

The buyer has Tianze in a browser tab alongside Ledes, Ctube, and one Alibaba seller. They're now doing detailed comparison. This is where most deals are won or lost.

**Q5 (Mark): "Show me the certificates. AS/NZS 2053? UL? ISO?"**
Now Mark wants the actual documents — not just a logo on the homepage. He needs to download a PDF he can show his business partner or forward to a customer who's asking. This is where Tianze's certification gap versus Ledes/Ctube becomes a real obstacle.

**Q6 (Mark): "What's your best price for a 20ft container of 25mm 90-degree bends?"**
Mark is doing landed-cost math. He needs: unit price at container quantity, container loading quantity, and enough info to estimate CIF to his port. He's comparing this against his current local wholesaler cost and against Alibaba quotes.

**Q7 (James): "What are the exact tolerances? Will this pass inspection on my job site?"**
James needs dimensional specs — OD, wall thickness, plus/minus tolerances — and he needs them presented like an engineering data sheet, not a marketing brochure. He's checking these against the project spec.

**Q8 (Mark): "What's the MOQ? Can I start with a trial order?"**
Mark doesn't want to commit to 5 containers sight unseen. He wants to know: what's the minimum I can order to test quality? Is a mixed container possible (multiple SKUs in one shipment)?

**Q9 (James): "Do you have test reports? Impact resistance, flame rating, UV resistance?"**
For commercial projects, James may need to submit supplier test data to the general contractor or building inspector. Third-party lab reports carry more weight than factory self-test data.

**Q10 (Mark): "Why should I buy from you instead of Ledes or Ctube who already have UL?"**
Mark won't say this directly, but it's what he's thinking. This is where the "we make the machines that make the pipes" differentiator either lands or doesn't. Mark needs a reason that's concrete, not just a tagline.

**Content needed to answer Stage 2:**
- Dedicated certifications/standards page with downloadable documents (ISO 9001, test reports, standards compliance evidence)
- Honest certification positioning per brand strategy Option A: "engineered to meet X standard, certification in progress, verify with free samples"
- Product detail pages with engineering-grade spec tables (dimensions, tolerances, materials, test data)
- Pricing guidance content (not public pricing, but: container loading charts, MOQ policy, "request quote for your quantity")
- Differentiator content: manufacturing process transparency — "we build our own bending machines" with photos/video as concrete proof
- Standards comparison table showing technical compliance even where formal certification is pending

---

### Stage 3: Decision (Ready to Act But Still Hesitant)

The buyer has decided Tianze is a serious option. But placing a first order with a new Chinese supplier is a commitment — money, time, reputation risk. These are the final-gate questions.

**Q11 (Mark): "Can I get samples before I commit to a container?"**
This is a commitment test in both directions. Mark wants to hold the product, check the quality, maybe show it to his biggest customer. He's also testing responsiveness — how fast do they ship samples? Do they charge? This signals how they'll behave as a long-term partner.

**Q12 (James): "What's your shortest lead time? I need this in 3 weeks, not 3 months."**
James has a project deadline. If Tianze can't deliver within the project window, nothing else matters. He needs a credible lead time — not a sales promise, but an honest range based on stock vs. production.

**Q13 (Mark): "What's the shipping arrangement? FOB Lianyungang? Can you do CIF to Melbourne?"**
Logistics details that Mark needs to finalize his landed-cost calculation and decide whether to use his own freight forwarder or Tianze's.

**Q14 (Mark): "What if quality drops after the first batch? What's your warranty / claims process?"**
Mark is protecting his business. If he sells Tianze fittings to his retail customers and quality fails, it's his reputation on the line. He needs to know: what happens when something goes wrong?

**Q15 (James): "Can you match this drawing exactly? What's the tooling lead time for custom molds?"**
For custom specs, James needs the final specifics: timeline for mold creation, minimum run after tooling, tolerance guarantees on custom pieces.

**Q16 (Mark): "Can you do our private label / brand packaging?"**
Mark wants to sell under his own brand. This question tests whether Tianze is flexible on packaging and labeling — important for distributors building their own brand identity.

**Q17 (Both): "Who else have you supplied? Any references in my market?"**
The final trust question. Buyers want evidence that someone like them has already taken the risk and it worked out. Case studies, reference customers (with permission), or even just "we export to 20+ countries" with some specificity.

**Content needed to answer Stage 3:**
- Clear sample policy (free samples, shipping timeline, how to request)
- Lead time information (stock items vs. production orders, realistic ranges)
- Shipping/logistics FAQ (FOB port, CIF options, container loading guidance)
- Quality assurance page or section (QC process, batch consistency, what happens if there's a defect)
- OEM/private label details (MOQ for custom packaging, artwork requirements)
- Customer evidence (export country count with regional specificity, case studies if available, factory visit invitation as ultimate trust signal)

## Content Gaps

### Gap 1: Certifications & Test Reports (Critical)

**Unanswered questions:** Q5, Q9
**Impact:** Blocks Stage 2 for both personas. Mark can't shortlist without certs. James can't proceed without test reports.
**What's needed:** A certifications section (on About page or standalone) with:
- Downloadable ISO 9001 certificate
- Standards compliance evidence (even pre-certification technical data)
- Third-party test reports (impact, flame, UV) where available
- Honest status of market-specific certifications (AS/NZS in progress)
**Competitive context:** Ledes and Ctube lead with cert logos. Tianze must compensate with transparency (per brand positioning Option A).

### Gap 2: Pricing & Trade Terms Guidance (Critical)

**Unanswered questions:** Q6, Q8 (partially), Q13
**Impact:** Mark cannot do landed-cost math — the core calculation that decides whether to switch suppliers.
**What's needed:**
- Container loading charts (how many 25mm bends fit in a 20ft/40ft container)
- MOQ policy clearly stated (minimum order, mixed container policy, trial order option)
- Shipping terms (FOB Lianyungang, CIF available, typical transit times to key markets)
- Payment terms overview (T/T, L/C, deposit structure)
**Note:** Exact unit pricing stays inquiry-based (industry standard). But giving the buyer enough to estimate is the gap.

### Gap 3: Quality Assurance & After-Sales (High)

**Unanswered questions:** Q14
**Impact:** Mark's final-gate blocker. Without knowing the claims process, he's taking uninsurable risk.
**What's needed:**
- QC process description (incoming material inspection, in-process checks, final inspection, batch sampling)
- What happens when a defect is found (replacement policy, timeline, who pays shipping)
- Quality consistency narrative — "batch #10 as good as batch #1" (Mark's words)
**Competitive context:** Most competitors don't address this either — it's an opportunity to differentiate.

### Gap 4: Engineering-Grade Product Specs (Medium)

**Unanswered questions:** Q7 (partially)
**Impact:** James can't verify spec compliance without tolerance data.
**What's needed:**
- Full dimensional specs per product: OD, ID, wall thickness, length, tolerances (plus/minus)
- Material properties: resin type, impact rating, flame rating, temperature range
- Presented as data tables (engineer format), not marketing copy
**Current state:** Product pages have some specs but depth and consistency vary by market.

### Gap 5: Competitive Differentiation Framing (Medium)

**Unanswered questions:** Q10
**Impact:** When Mark has Ledes and Ctube in adjacent tabs, "we make the machines" needs to land as a concrete advantage, not just a tagline.
**What's needed:**
- Explicit "why Tianze" content that connects bending machine manufacturing to buyer outcomes:
  - "We control quality at the machine level" (not just at the inspection level)
  - "Hot-bending = ~50% cost savings vs injection molding" (pass some to buyer)
  - "We test every product on our own machines" (self-validated quality loop)
- This content exists in fragments (ChainSection on homepage) but isn't structured as an answer to the competitive comparison question.

### Gap 6: Lead Time & Logistics Detail (Medium)

**Unanswered questions:** Q12, Q13
**Impact:** James's timeline question is a deal-breaker. Mark needs logistics to finalize cost.
**What's needed:**
- Lead time ranges: stock items (X days) vs. production orders (X-Y weeks)
- Shipping: FOB port (Lianyungang), typical transit times to Australia/SEA/Middle East/North America
- Container loading reference (ties to Gap 2)

## Priority Recommendation

Based on question frequency, competitive impact, and deal-blocking potential:

1. **Certifications & Test Reports** (Gap 1) — blocks both personas at Stage 2
2. **Pricing & Trade Terms** (Gap 2) — blocks Mark's core decision math
3. **Quality Assurance** (Gap 3) — Mark's final-gate blocker
4. **Engineering Specs** (Gap 4) — James's comparison blocker
5. **Competitive Differentiation** (Gap 5) — wins Stage 2 against Ledes/Ctube
6. **Logistics Detail** (Gap 6) — operational but not differentiating
