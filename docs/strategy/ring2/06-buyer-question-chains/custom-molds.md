# Buyer Question Chain: Custom Molds / OEM

> Ring 2, Task 6 | Sub-line: Custom Molds / OEM Manufacturing
> Input: Ring 1 — OEM Buyer persona (Sarah), competitive analysis, brand positioning (Line 3)

## Personas Covered

- **OEM Buyer (Sarah)** — Product Manager or Sourcing Director at a regional building materials brand (20-100 employees). The company owns the brand and distribution but outsources manufacturing. Sarah's job: find a capable Chinese factory that can produce PVC fittings to her company's specifications, under their brand, with the flexibility to handle non-standard sizes and the reliability to maintain quality across repeat orders.

**Core relationship model:** This is a long-term manufacturing partnership, not a one-time purchase. Sarah is evaluating whether Tianze can become her company's production arm for PVC conduit fittings. Every question she asks is filtered through: "Can I trust this factory with my product line?"

## Question Chain

### Stage 1: Awareness

> Trigger: New product line launch, or current OEM supplier can't meet a new spec requirement.
> Channels: Alibaba, trade shows, industry referrals, Google ("PVC conduit OEM manufacturer China").
> Mindset: Screening. Sarah is building a shortlist — she needs quick signals that Tianze is worth a deeper look.

| # | Question | What she's really asking | Seed |
|---|----------|--------------------------|------|
| A1 | "Do you do OEM / custom manufacturing?" | Is this even a service you offer, or are you just a catalog seller? | -- |
| A2 | "Can you develop molds from our CAD drawings?" | Do you have in-house mold development, or do you outsource tooling? Can you turn my design file into a physical product? | Mold development capability |
| A3 | "What types of custom fittings can you produce?" | What's the range of geometries, materials, and sizes you can handle? Are there things you can't do? | Mold development capability |
| A4 | "Do you have your own factory or are you a trading company?" | The fundamental trust question for any B2B buyer sourcing from China. | -- |
| A5 | "What's your minimum order quantity for custom products?" | Can I start with a small trial batch (5,000 pcs), or do you force me into 50,000? | MOQ flexibility |

**Key insight:** At Awareness, Sarah is not comparing suppliers yet — she is filtering. Questions are binary (yes/no capable). The site must answer A1-A5 within the first scroll of the OEM page, or Sarah bounces.

**Tianze advantage at this stage:** The "we make the machines that make the pipes" narrative immediately answers A4 with overwhelming proof. No trading company manufactures its own bending equipment. This is a filter-passing signal that competitors cannot replicate.

> Mindset: Sarah has narrowed to 1-2 factories. She's now managing internal stakeholders: QA wants testing data, Legal wants IP protection, Marketing wants packaging specs. Her questions reflect her internal approval process.

**Key insight:** At Decision, Sarah is no longer evaluating capability — she's evaluating trustworthiness and partnership readiness. Every question maps to an internal stakeholder she needs to convince. Content must be "forwardable": Sarah needs to send your NDA policy page to Legal, your QC documentation page to QA.

**Tianze advantage at this stage:** Factory visit invitation is a powerful commitment mechanism (competitive analysis: no competitor explicitly offers this). "Come see the machines we built" is an invitation trading companies cannot extend.

## Content Gaps

### Gap 1: IP Protection (Critical)

**Missing entirely.** No mention of NDA availability, design exclusivity, or mold ownership policy anywhere on the site. For OEM buyers, IP protection is a deal-breaker — it's in Sarah's top 3 concerns. A brand will not share CAD files with a factory that has no visible IP protection commitment.

**Recommendation:** Add a dedicated "IP Protection" section to `/oem-custom-manufacturing` covering:
- NDA available (standard practice, not special request)
- Mold ownership policy (buyer pays for tooling = buyer owns the mold)
- Design exclusivity options
- Confidentiality in communication and sample handling

### Gap 2: MOQ Flexibility & Scaling Path (Critical)

**Implied but not explicit.** The process flow shows "Trial Run" as a step, but there's no stated minimum order quantity, no "start small, scale later" language, and no pricing model for first-order vs. repeat-order. Sarah's #2 concern ("We need 5,000 pieces first, not 50,000") has no direct answer.

**Recommendation:** Add a "Flexible Order Quantities" section or FAQ entry with:
- Trial order MOQ (e.g., "as low as X pieces for custom products")
- Scaling path: trial > first production > ongoing partnership
- Explicit language: "We support graduated volumes — start with a trial batch and scale as your market responds"

### Gap 3: OEM References / Case Studies (High)

**No social proof for OEM capability.** The site shows no evidence that Tianze has successfully completed OEM projects for other brands. Even anonymized references ("We produce private-label conduit fittings for a North American distributor") would significantly reduce perceived risk.

**Recommendation:** Create 2-3 anonymized OEM case studies for the site (blog or dedicated section). Structure: challenge > solution > result. No need to name the client — the specificity of the project details provides credibility.

### Gap 4: Mold Development Detail (Medium)

**Surface-level coverage.** The current "Mold Development" scope card says "In-house mold design and manufacturing for any fitting geometry" — one sentence. An OEM buyer sending a CAD file needs to understand: What format do you accept? What's the tolerance? How many revision rounds? What's the cost structure?

**Recommendation:** Expand mold development into a richer content block:
- Accepted file formats (STEP, IGES, DWG, etc.)
- Typical tolerance range
- Revision policy (e.g., "2 rounds included, additional rounds at cost")
- Timeline breakdown: design review > mold cutting > sample > approval

### Gap 5: QC Documentation (Medium)

**Not addressed for OEM context.** Quality assurance is listed as a service scope item, but there's no detail on what documentation an OEM buyer receives per batch. QA teams at buyer companies need specific deliverables: dimensional inspection reports, material certificates, test results.

**Recommendation:** Add QC documentation section listing:
- Per-batch deliverables (inspection report, material cert, photos)
- Optional third-party inspection support
- Defect rate commitment and handling process

### Gap 6: Factory Visit Invitation (Medium)

**Missing commitment mechanism.** Competitive analysis identified that no competitor explicitly invites factory visits. This is a low-cost, high-trust action that Tianze can own. Currently, a buyer would need to proactively request a visit through the contact form — there's no invitation.

**Recommendation:** Add factory visit CTA to `/oem-custom-manufacturing`:
- "Visit our factory in Lianyungang — see the machines we built and the production lines they run."
- Combine with free sample offer for a dual commitment mechanism
